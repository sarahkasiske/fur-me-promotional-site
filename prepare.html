Prepare for Your Dog

Whether your dog is from a shelter or breeder, adapting to a new environment and family is a new experience. Approach the first few days with patience, making your dog feel welcome while giving them space and time to adapt. Offering them some clothing or a blanket that smells like you can make a new dog feel more secure.

Dog Proof Your Home

One of the last things a new dog owner thinks about is puppy proofing your home. Many foods that are common place for human consumption can be dangerous for dogs. It is best to stick with a dog food diet for your pet.

Some plants and household cleaners are particularly bad for dogs. Never take it for granted that dogs understand it is bad to eat chocolate or toilet bowl cleaner, what a dog chooses to eat may shock you. Also watch how your dog plays with toys. Some dogs are aggressive chewers and can destroy a dog toy, swallowing parts of it in a matter of minutes.

Things that are Dangerous to Dogs

Foods

  • Avocado
  • Chocolate and Caffeine
  • Coconut and Coconut Oil
  • Alcohol
  • Macadamia Nuts
  • Grapes and Raisins

Plants

  • American Holly
  • Elephant Ears
  • Chamomile
  • Bird of Paradise
  • Chives
  • Yellow Oleander

Household Items

  • Vinegar and Water
  • Breath Fresheners
  • Ibuprofen and Naproxen
  • Kaopectate and Pepto Bismol
  • Mosquito Repellent
  • Bar Soap and Face Wash

What your dog might be going through

When you bring your dog home remember this is a new environment. These major changes would make anyone stressed. Be patient, give your dog space to warm up to her new family. She will quickly feel comfortable in her new surroundings.

How to make your dog more comfortable

Ask your breeder or shelter for a toy they have played with in the past. Arrange a bed for them with one of your old t-shirts or blankets so they know what you smell like and can get comfortable with you. Give them a space where they can go and not be bothered by you or anyone else.

Socialization

Socialization is how you introduce your puppy to the world, its sights and sounds. It is important to start early, but important for dogs of all ages. Introduce your puppy to things at their own pace, allowing them to decide when to approach and when to retreat.Expose your puppy to new sights, sounds and experiences, people laughing, cars honking, vacuums and doorbells. Walking on a busy street, greeting people and experiencing bicycles and baby strollers are all part of socialization. Before your dog arrives make a plan for how you will socialize it.
